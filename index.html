Correct answer: Increased β-hCG

2) A 5 year old boy presents to the GP with frequent chest infections and stomach pains. He is evidently short of breath and has been coughing repeatedly. His mother mentions that his stool appears light coloured, oily and floats in the toilet bowl. What is the most appropriate option as a first line and immediate investigation for this patient?

The most likely diagnosis is cystic fibrosis. When presented in the community, the firstline investigation is a chloride sweat test due to its high specificity. Sweat is triggered by pilocarpine (a muscarinic agonist) and will be unusually high in chloride levels due to CFTR dysfunction.

Genetic testing would be more definitive but is not a standard firstline investigation.

The heel prick blood test is done for newborn babies at 5 days. This child is 5 years too old.

A high resolution CT thorax is not indicated in cystic fibrosis. It can be indicated for cases of idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis.

3) The investigation comes back positive for cystic fibrosis. What is the most likely gene mutation that has occurred

The most common mutation in cystic fibrosis is del F508 which is a deletion of a triplet of base pairs on chromosome 7 resulting in the loss of phenylalanine from the CFTR protein. The dysfunctional CFTR protein is unable to travel to the cell surface.

4) A known HIV-positive patient comes to the hospital for a routine blood test. He has poor adherence to his ART therapy and his CD4 count is 145 cells/µL. The doctor would like to begin antibiotic prophylaxis. Which medication is most likely initiated?

TMP-SMX is first line for prophylaxis against opportunistic infections in patients with poorly controlled HIV and a CD4 count < 200 cells/µL. The most relevant opportunistic infection at this CD4 count is Pneumocystis jirovecii, as it can cause pneumonia and high mortality.

Although Pneumocystis jirovecii is a fungus, TMP-SMX is able to inhibit its de novo folate synthesis.

Since Pneumocystis jirovecii is a fungus, penicillins such as amoxicillin and piperacillin will not be effective.

Pneumocystis also has 80S ribosomes instead of 50s so macrolides like clarithromycin are also ineffective.

Tazobactam and piperacillin are a combination of antibiotics commonly used in sepsis.

5) A medical student is learning about the circulation and breathing mechanism in a foetus. Which of the following statements are true?

1. The ductus venosus bypasses the lungs and allows for deoxygenated to move straight from the pulmonary artery to the ascending aorta
2. There are two umbilical arteries and only one umbilical vein
3. The foramen ovale closes after the baby’s first breath to become the fossa ovalis

Statement 1 is incorrect because the ductus venosus bypasses the liver, not the lungs. This is done by the ductus arteriosus, connecting the pulmonary artery to the aorta.

Recall that the umbilical vein confusingly brings oxygenated blood to the fetus (from the placenta) and the umbilical arteries bring deoxygenated blood back to the mother. This is because they technically belong to the fetus and thus are named appropriately to whether they are travelling to or from the fetus' heart.

If the foramen ovale does not close, it can lead to PFO (Patent Foramen Ovale) which can be asymptomatic or cause paradoxical embolisms.

6) An 18 year old girl comes into the GP explaining how she feels concerned about a notable lack of breast development. After further questioning she reveals that she has not yet began to menstruate. She has webbing skin between the neck and shoulders and a short stature. Which genetic karyotype is most likely to cause this presentation?

45 XO is the karyotype known as Turner’s syndrome. It is the most common cause of primary amenorrhea and physically present with a short stature, webbed neck and low set ears. There are no preventive measures available for this condition. Hormone replacement of growth hormone, oestrogen and progesterone can provide some symptomatic management.

46 XX is the normal karyotype for a woman.

47 XYY is Jacobs syndrome. Individuals are male and can be asymptomatic. Those that present with symptoms can be taller than average and can have delayed intellectual development.

47 XXY is Klinefelter’s syndrome. Individuals are male and are often taller than normal with longer limbs. They can have delayed or incomplete sexual organ development with decreased pubic hair and potential gynaecomastia.

7) Constipation is very common during pregnancy as early as the first trimester and is often worse around the second or third month. What is the physiological mechanism behind constipation during pregnancy?

The surge in βhCG is strongly associated to morning sickness.

Oestrogen does not have a large role in bowel motility during pregnancy and neither does increased RBC production.

9) Which term best describes a new, irreversible, and autonomous proliferation of cells, which persists independently of the original stimulus and is seen in tumours?

Neoplasia is the final stage of a tumour becoming a carcinoma. It is the new, irreversible, autonomous proliferation of cells that can be well differentiated or abnormal. This means neoplasia can be benign or malignant.

Hyperplasia is the proliferation of the number of cells and is not always pathological (e.g. thickening of the uterus in the luteal phase)

Metaplasia is the reversible changing of cell type with another cell type. For example, GERD can cause stratified squamous cells to be replaced by columnar epithelium in a condition known as Barrett’s oesophagus.

Dysplasia is the precancerous disordered growth and maturation of epithelial cells. It is potentially reversible if the inciting stimulus is removed. Think of it as “just before cancer”.

10) Once an antibody attaches to a surface antigen on a bacteria, which region of the antibody interacts with phagocytes such as macrophages and neutrophils to initiate phagocytosis?

The Fc region is the stem of the antibody that phagocytes can bind to. It’s the end that is always shaped like a stick/line.

The Fab region is the region that binds to the antigen, consisting of one light chain and a portion of a heavy chain.

The hinge region is a stretch of heavy chains between Fc and Fab regions that looks like a hinge.

The variable region is the part of an antibody's polypeptide chain that has a unique amino acid sequence and forms the antigen-binding site

11) A 12 year old boy lands on an outstretched hand in an attempt to break his fall whilst skateboarding. The clinician identifies a “dinner fork” deformity and comes to the diagnosis of a Colles Fracture. Which of the following locations is this fracture present?

A Colles fracture is a fracture of the distal radius, most commonly caused due to a fall of the outstretched hand. This topic appeared in my sem test 2 years ago.

Note the 5th metacarpal and trapezium are not near each other, but a fracture of the 5th metacarpal is common in boxing.

A fracture of the proximal radius (usually of the radial head) is a common fracture of the elbow, particularly in children and the elderly. This can also be caused by falling on an outstretched hand but the dinner fork abnormality is pathognomonic for a Colles fracture.

A fracture of the distal humerus accounts for 30% of elbow fractures.

12) The pudendal nerve exits the pelvic cavity via which foramen?

The pudendal nerve exits the pelvic cavity via the greater sciatic foramen, below the piriformis muscles. It then reenters via the lesser sciatic foramen to innervate the pelvic floor muscles, sphincter muscles and provide sensation to the perineum.

The obturator foramen is positioned anteriorly (compared to the posterior sciatic foramina) and contains the obturator nerve and membrane.

The pelvic brim is an anatomical landmark marking the boundary between the true and false pelvis. It is not a foramen.

19) A combination of mifepristone and misoprostol can be taken for a medical abortion. What is the mechanism of action of mifepristone?

21) A 40 year old man undergoes genetic screening and finds out he is a carrier of the cystic fibrosis gene. His wife’s mother had cystic fibrosis but his wife does not. What is the percentage chance that their child will have cystic fibrosis?

25% is correct because CF is autosomal recessive. There is a 50% chance that the heterozygous father passes on his CF allele and his wife must also be heterozygous if her mother had CF. Therefore if both parents are heterozygous for the CF gene, there is a 25% chance that both parents pass on the CF allele.

34) A child is able to use symbols like words and objects to represent other things and can pretend play with cars and stuffed toy animals. However, they are unable to see things from other people’s points of view and believe that everyone thinks the same way they do. They also believe that a tall skinny glass of juice contains more that a short wide one, even if the amount of juice is identical. Which stage of Piaget’s stages of cognitive development is this child currently in?

This child is in the preoperational stage as they are able symbolise and play, but they still show egocentrism and do not understand conservation. This stage last from ages 2-7.

Sensorimotor stage begins from ages 0-2 and is when a baby learns to coordinate their body and focus on physical sensations. Key things they learn in this stage include object permanence and self recognition.

Concrete operational stage spans 7-11. Children understand conversation and can empathise other people’s feelings.

Formal operational stage follows on, but everyone may not always reach this stage. People begin to think more abstractly and deal with hypothetical problems.

35) A 23 year old woman who is confirmed to not have cystic fibrosis wants to know whether she is a carrier of the CF gene. She is concerned about this because her biological brother has cystic fibrosis. Neither of her parents suffer from cystic fibrosis. What is the probability that she is a carrier of the CF gene?

This is a tricky inheritance pattern question. We know CF has an autosomal recessive inheritance. For her brother to have CF and her parents to not, both parents must be heterozygous carriers.

We know that the woman does not have CF. Therefore she is not homozygous for the CF gene. This leaves 3 options:

  • homozygous no gene
  • carrier, CF gene inherited from mother
  • carrier, CF gene inherited from father

These all have equal chance so there is a 2/3 chance that she is a carrier.

36) A D-dimer test can be used to detect for a pulmonary embolism (blood clot in the lung). D-dimer’s use a measure of “fibrin degradation products” after a clot forms. A pulmonary embolism almost always results in an elevated D-dimer result. However, it is not very conclusive on its own as many other conditions can cause an elevated D-dimer. Which of the following statements about D-dimer’s are true?

D-dimer’s have a high sensitivity but low specificity.

Recall sensitivity = proportion of those with the disease who correctly receive a positive result, and specificity = proportion of people without the disease who correctly receive a negative result.

Therefore it is used as a “rule out test”; a negative D-dimer is useful in ruling out PE (pulmonary embolism).

D-dimer’s have a low PPV and a high NPV.

38) Which of the following is a first-line treatment for osteoporosis?

Alendronate is an oral bisphosphonate that is a cost-effective first-line treatment for osteoporosis, often given alongside calcium and vitamin D supplements. Other examples of bisphosphonates include risedronate and zoledronic acid (IV). Bisphosphonates work by binding to hydroxyapatite in bone.

Denosumab is a RANKL inhibitor and is generally considered second or third-line for osteoporosis.

Prednisolone is a corticosteroid that will worsen osteoporosis by increasing osteoclast activity and inhibiting bone formation by osteoblasts.

Tamoxifen is an oestrogen receptor blocker used in ER positive breast cancer. Its effects on oestrogen are different in other tissues however, so it is unclear whether it preserves or increases bone loss; studies have shown this differs in whether the woman is premenopausal or postmenopausal.

39) Which of the following examination findings in a 50 year old woman are most likely to be breast cancer?

Features that can indicate malignancy in breast lumps include irregular shape, hard/firm consistency, fixation to skin (immobility), changes over time, nontenderness, skin changes (such as peau d’orange), bloodstained discharge and nipple retraction.

Therefore a hard immovable lump with an irregular border, with some skin changes and is nontender upon touch would raise suspicions and warrant further investigation of lymph nodes and a mammogram.

A soft smooth movable lump on the underside of the breast that is nontender and rubbery in texture is likely to be a breast cyst or a lipoma.

A warm, red, swollen, lump that is tender upon touch is likely to be mastitis or potentially a breast abscess.

A smooth, firm, highly movable lump found in the upper outer quadrant of the breast is most likely to be a fibroadenoma. This is the most common cause of a breast lump and although the upper outer quadrant is the most common location of breast cancer, it is the most common location of a fibroadenoma too.

49) Which cells are the first in the spermatogenesis lineage to become haploid cells?

The process of meiosis I is where chromosome number halves and haploid cells are first seen. Diploid primary spermatocytes (2n) undergo meiosis I to produce haploid secondary spermatocytes (1n). These then undergo meiosis II to produce double the number of spermatids (1n) which mature into spermatozoa (sperm cells).
